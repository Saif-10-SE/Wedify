However, there is no centralized system in place to track the availability and usage of chemicals across the organization. There are some sensitive dual usage chemicals, for example chemical named ephedrine is used in drugs to treat hypotension but it can also be used to make heroine, an infamous illegal narcotic. Government also need Pharmaceutical companies to submit the report of usage of sensitive chemicals in the company in order to avoid misuse of these sensitive chemicals.
[400] There is a need to develop a Chemical tracking system. â€œThe system should keep track of all the chemical containers that company already have in the stockroom and in laboratories. In this way, the chemists can get some chemicals from someone down the hall instead of always buying a new container. This should save us a lot of money. Also, the Health and Safety Department needs to generate government reports on chemical usage and disposal with a lot less work than it takes them today.
[401] The company will route orders to buy chemicals from Chemists or Pharmacists to vendors (companies who supply the chemicals) for fulfillment, the vendors will send chemical containers and invoices to Contoso Pharmaceuticals, and Contosoâ€™s purchasing department will pay the vendors. [403] A Chemical Tracking System is essential for addressing these challenges. The proposed system will maintain a real-time record of chemical inventory in stockrooms and labs, improve internal visibility, reduce redundant purchases, and facilitate easy generation of compliance reports.

It identifies external entities (also called terminators) outside the system that interface to it in some way, as well as data, control, and material flows between the terminators and the system. The context diagram is the top level in a data flow diagram developed according to the principles of structured analysis.

[681] Sprint:
[682] In Agile project management, a sprint is a fixed-length period during which a team works to complete a set of tasks and deliver a potentially shippable increment of product. Sprints are typically one to four weeks long, but two to three weeks is a common duration.
[683] Epic:
[684] An epic is a user story that is too large to fully implement in a single iteration. Its a multi-sprint project goal or a broad area of functionality that requires significant development effort.  Because epics span iterations, they must be split into sets of smaller stories. Sometimes epics are large enough that they must be subdivided into multiple epics, each of which is then split into multiple stories until each resulting story can be reliably estimated and then implemented and tested within a single iteration. Breaking epics down into smaller epics and then into user stories is often referred to as story decomposition
[685] Scope:
[686] Covers multiple releases or iterations and often spans across several teams.
[687] Purpose:
[688] Provides a high-level overview of the project and helps in prioritizing and planning the release.
[689] Example:
[690] "Implement e-commerce functionality".
[691] Feature:
[692] A specific piece of functionality or a deliverable within an Epic that provides value to the user.
[693] Scope:
[694] Smaller than an Epic and can be developed and released in a single iteration.
[695] Purpose:
[696] Breaks down the Epic into manageable chunks and guides the development process.
[697] Example:
[698] "Product listing page," "Order page," "Payment page" within the "e-commerce functionality" Epic.
[699] User Story:
[700] A user story is a â€œshort, simple description of a feature told from the perspective of the person who desires the new capability, usually a user or customer of the systemâ€. User stories are sized so as to be fully implementable in a single iteration. Each completed user story iteratively builds the feature. User stories often are written according to the following template, although other styles also are used:
[701] As a <type of user>, I want <some goal> so that <some reason OR Benefit>.
[702] Scope:
[703] The smallest unit of work that can be delivered within a sprint.
[704] Purpose:
[705] Captures the requirements of a feature in a way that is easy for developers to understand and implement.
[706] Example:
[707] "As a user, I want to add items to my shopping cart so that I can buy them later"

[710] Acceptance Criteria
[711] A set of conditions or rules that a user story must satisfy to be considered "done" and accepted by the product owner.
[712] Purpose:
[713] To define what success looks like for a user story.
[714] Written by:
[715] Usually written by the product owner, sometimes with input from QA and developers.
[716] Format:

[726] Story Point:
[727] In Agile, story points are a relative unit of measurement used to estimate the effort required to complete a user story or other piece of work. They are not time-based but rather represent the complexity and effort relative to other items on the backlog.
[728] Key aspects of story points range in Agile:
[729] Relative, not absolute:
[730] Story points are assigned relative to other tasks, meaning a story with 2 points is roughly twice as complex as a story with 1 point.
[731] No specific time value:
[732] Story points don't directly equate to hours or days; they represent a degree of effort or complexity.
[733] Common scales:
[734] Teams often use scales like Fibonacci (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...)
[735] Benefits:
[736] Story points help teams estimate work, plan sprints, and manage expectations, rather than focusing on individual time estimates.

[738] Backlog grooming:
[739] It is a process where the product owner and development team collaboratively review and refine the product backlog. It is a crucial practice in Agile project management that involves regularly updating and refining the product backlog. It focuses on ensuring the backlog is well-defined, prioritized, and ready for upcoming sprint cycles, ultimately contributing to a more efficient and effective development process.
[740] This includes activities like:
[741] Refining user stories: Making sure they are clear, concise, and easy to understand.
[742] Breaking down large items: Dividing complex tasks into smaller, more manageable units.
[743] Estimating effort: Assessing how much work each item will require.
[744] Prioritizing items: Determining which tasks should be tackled first.
[745] Adding new items: Incorporating newly discovered requirements or features.
[746] Removing outdated items: Eliminating items that are no longer relevant.
[747] Why it's important:
[748] Improves sprint planning:
[749] A well-groomed backlog allows the team to effectively plan and execute sprints.
[750] Enhances collaboration:
[751] The process promotes communication and collaboration between the product owner and development team.
[752] Increases predictability:
[753] By refining the backlog, the team can better predict the scope and effort required for upcoming sprints.
[754] Keeps the backlog up-to-date:
[755] Ensures the backlog reflects the latest needs and priorities of the product.
[756] Benefits:
[757] Increased velocity:
[758] By optimizing the backlog, the team can work more efficiently and deliver more value.
[759] Reduced risk:
[760] A well-defined backlog helps to minimize the risk of scope creep and delays.
[761] Improved team morale:
[762] When the team understands what they are working on and why, they are more motivated and engaged

[1908] ERD
[1909] We now present another example, a UNIVERSITY database, [3] to illustrate the ER modeling concepts. Suppose that a database is needed to keep track of student enrollments in classes and studentsâ€™ final grades. After analyzing the miniworld rules and the usersâ€™ needs, the requirements for this database were determined to be as follows (for brevity, we show the chosen entity type names and attribute names for the conceptual schema in parentheses as we describe the requirements; relationship type names are only shown in the ER schema diagram):
[1910] The university is organized into colleges (COLLEGE), and each college has a unique name (CName), a main office (COffice) and phone (CPhone), and a particular faculty member who is dean of the college. Each college administers a number of academic departments (DEPT). Each department has a unique name (DName), a unique code number (DCode), a main office (DOffice) and phone (DPhone), and a particular faculty member who chairs the department. We keep track of the start date (CStartDate) when that faculty member began chairing the department.
[1911] A department offers a number of courses (COURSE), each of which has a unique course name (CoName), a unique code number (CCode), a course level (Level: this can be coded as 1 for freshman level, 2 for sophomore, 3 for junior, 4 for senior, 5 for MS level, and 6 for PhD level), a course credit hours (Credits), and a course description (CDesc). The database also keeps track of instructors (INSTRUCTOR); and each instructor has a unique identifier (Id), name (IName), office (IOffice), phone (IPhone), and rank (Rank);
[1912] in addition, each instructor works for one primary academic department.
[1913] The database will keep student data (STUDENT) and stores each studentâ€™s name (SName, composed of first name (FName), middle name (MName), last name (LName)), student id (Sid, unique for every student), address (Addr), phone (Phone), major code (Major), and date of birth (DoB). A student is assigned to one primary academic department. It is required to keep track of the studentâ€™s grades in each section the student has completed.
[1914] Courses are offered as sections (SECTION). Each section is related to a single course and a single instructor and has a unique section identifier (SecId). A section also has a section number (SecNo: this is coded as 1, 2, 3, . . . for multiple sections offered during the same semester/year), semester (Sem), year (Year), classroom (CRoom: this is coded as a combination of building code (Bldg) and room number (RoomNo) within the building), and days/times (DaysTime: for example, â€˜MWF 9am-9.50amâ€™ or â€˜TR 3.30pm-5.20pmâ€™â€” restricted to only allowed days/time values). (Note: The database will keep track of all the sections offered for the past several years, in addition to the current offerings. The SecId is unique for all sections, not just the sections for a particular semester.) The database keeps track of the students in each section, and the grade is recorded when available (this is a many-to-many relationship between students and sections). A section must have at least five students.

[1922] A data dictionary [1] is a collection of detailed information about the data entities used in an application. Collecting the information about composition, data types, allowed values, and the like into a shared resource identifies the data validation criteria, helps developers write programs correctly, and minimizes integration problems. The data dictionary complements the project glossary, which defines application domain or business terms, abbreviations, and acronyms. We recommend keeping the data dictionary and the glossary separate.This information feeds into design in the form of database schemas, tables, and attributes, which ultimately lead to variable names in programs. The time you invest in creating a data dictionary will be more than repaid by avoiding the mistakes that can result when project participants have different understandings of the data.
